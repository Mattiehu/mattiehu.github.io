Motherhood in Crisis

Motherhood in Crisis, published by Al Jazeera and created by Laurence Ivil, Alicia Prager, and Saidu Bah, is a graphic novel documenting the experiences of four women navigating pregnancy and childbirth in Sierra Leone, where healthcare access remains severely limited and maternal mortality rates are among the highest in the world.

The statistic that unsettled me most was that one in ten women in Sierra Leone die during childbirth. I had understood, in a general sense, that giving birth carries risk, but I had not previously encountered a figure this severe. Reading it within the context of four real, named individuals made the number feel less like data and more like a pattern of loss that had been quietly unfolding for years. It shifted something in how I understood the scale of the problem.

The choice to present this material as a graphic novel deserves attention. Serious journalism has long relied on written text as its primary medium, yet this project suggests that format itself shapes how an audience receives difficult information. For readers who engage more readily with visual content, illustrated panels offer a different kind of entry point into a story. Rather than processing facts at a distance, the reader follows a character’s face, posture, and movement through each moment. The pacing slows down in a way that written prose rarely enforces. I found myself spending more time with each scene than I typically would with a comparable article, which may reflect how effectively the format sustains attention across difficult subject matter.

Among the four stories, Heela’s was the one I returned to most. She was a student whose future narrowed significantly before she had any real opportunity to shape it herself. What made her story particularly affecting was not any single dramatic event, but the accumulation of circumstances that left her with very little room to choose otherwise. There is something specific about watching a young person’s possibilities contract so early, and the graphic novel format rendered that gradual foreclosure in a way that felt precise rather than sensationalized.

Projects like this one raise broader questions about what journalism can look like when it prioritizes human specificity over generalisation. By grounding systemic issues in individual testimony and pairing that testimony with illustration, the creators produced something that functions both as reportage and as an emotionally legible account of lives that might otherwise remain invisible to outside audiences.
